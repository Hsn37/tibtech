Multimodal Vision Framework for Behavior-based Precision Livestock Farming

June 18, 2024

By Taha, Qazi & Asim
Tibbling Technologies

The fusion of computer vision and deep learning has revolutionized non-contact animal monitoring systems, becoming a cornerstone in modern agriculture. These advanced technologies are pivotal for quantifying animal behavior and detecting diseases early, essential aspects of precision livestock farming that ensure animal health and welfare. Traditional methods for evaluating farm animal behavior, such as direct observation and physiological measurements, are often disruptive, subjective, and impractical for large-scale application. These methods also lack scientifically validated metrics to accurately assess animal well-being. Current animal welfare standards focus mainly on negative indicators like harm and stress, overlooking the importance of recognizing and promoting positive states crucial for both animal health and farm productivity.


Non-invasive monitoring systems present a promising solution to these challenges. By leveraging stress-minimizing and unbiased technologies, we can gather reliable data on animal welfare, potentially improving both livestock lives and agricultural outputs. Within this domain, pose estimation and instance segmentation are foundational techniques driving efforts to create robust and efficient models for behavioral analysis. While pose estimation offers in-depth insights through extensive datasets across diverse species, instance segmentation provides analytics beyond posture analysis.

Introducing AnimalFormer

We are excited to introduce AnimalFormer, a groundbreaking non-contact, multimodal AI framework designed for comprehensive livestock behavior analysis. Leveraging state-of-the-art models, AnimalFormer offers a holistic view of animal activities such as grazing, running, and resting, using an open-source sheep dataset. This innovative framework combines advanced techniques like ViTPose, GroundingDINO, and HQSAM for nuanced pose estimation and precise segmentation, capturing detailed movements and interactions.


Figure 1: Our integrated analysis framework, designed for comprehensive animal behavioral understanding demonstrated on a sheep dataset. This block diagram provides a clear overview of the data flow and processing steps within our end-to-end solution.

AnimalFormer provides a scalable solution adaptable to various livestock species and farm sizes, delivering critical insights for optimizing feed efficiency, monitoring health, and improving breeding programs. This novel approach transcends existing methodologies, facilitating precision livestock farming and proactive health monitoring through raw video data, without the need for invasive tagging or interaction. The non-invasive nature of AnimalFormer ensures minimal stress to animals, leading to more accurate observations and a deeper understanding of their well-being.


By harnessing the latest cutting-edge technology, AnimalFormer sets a new benchmark for animal welfare and farm management practices, contributing significantly to the advancement of precision livestock farming on a global scale.

Multimodal AI driven Livestock Behavior

Our detailed analysis of a sheep dataset showcases the capabilities of our AnimalFormer framework, focusing on gait, running, grazing, and resting behaviors. Through precise data-driven insights, we illustrate how such comprehensive monitoring can enhance understanding and management of livestock behaviors, serving as a model for broader applications in precision farming.

Figure 2: Qualitative outputs of our framework depicting running frames with keypoints and bounding boxes illustrating movement dynamics.

Figure 3: A. UMAP representation of the unique gait patterns of the sheep extracted from their running videos. B. Speed profile of the animal extracted at the commencement, midpoint, and conclusion of their running videos. C. The unique clusters of existing sheep gait patterns.

Sheep, as prey animals, often exhibit flight responses, making running a common behavior that can reveal much about their gaits and speed. By analyzing running videos of individual sheep, we can extract various poses using UMAP embeddings, as illustrated in Figure 3 (A). Each color in the figure represents poses of different animals, showing that while most poses cluster closely, some are more dispersed.


We validate this observation by clustering the poses into 10 unique groups (Figure 3 (C)). Despite the independence of running patterns among sheep, some share gait patterns, evidenced by overlapping clusters. Figure 3 (D) shows individual sheep’s poses within these clusters. For instance, sheep IDs 025 and 011 have most poses within clusters 9 and 1, respectively. Even sheep with dynamic gait patterns, like ID 014, exhibit most poses in a primary cluster with a few spread across others.


Pose estimation alone offers insights into gait patterns, but speed analysis is crucial for assessing health. Figure 3 (B) displays speed trends at different intervals, showing that sheep with fewer dynamic gait patterns tend to run faster. For example, ID 025, with limited poses, is the fastest, whereas ID 014, with many poses, is slower. This suggests a trade-off between speed and movement variety, aligning with the principles of locomotive efficiency, where consistent movements enhance speed.


Understanding gait diversity and speed has practical implications, such as identifying health issues and optimizing herd management, making this research valuable for both academic and agricultural applications.

Figure 2: Qualitative outputs of our framework depicting Grazing frames showcasing sheep engaged in feeding with extracted poses and segmentation masks highlighting the focus areas.

Figure 3: The grazing activity of the sheep in herds vs single

Using advanced animal detection and pose estimation, we analyzed and compared the grazing patterns of sheep in isolated settings versus when they are in a flock. Our findings, illustrated in Figure 3, reveal that sheep exhibit more substantial grazing activity when isolated compared to when they are part of a herd. This increased grazing in solitude is attributed to the absence of social stimuli and potential threats, allowing sheep to focus more intently on feeding.


In controlled environments devoid of predators or environmental stressors, sheep show reduced vigilance behavior, which is typically heightened in group settings. Our observations support this principle, indicating that solitary sheep take advantage of safety and tranquility to maximize food intake. In contrast, when sheep graze in groups, their feeding is often interrupted by social interactions such as establishing hierarchies or bonding, leading to less overall food consumption.


Quantitative data from our analysis clearly contrasts the two scenarios: isolated sheep show consistent feeding patterns with minimal interruptions, while herd grazing patterns are interspersed with non-feeding activities. This study provides valuable insights into the grazing behaviors of domestic sheep, which can help enhance farm management practices and optimize feeding strategies to improve animal well-being and productivity.


In summary, our analysis indicates that in controlled scenarios, sheep graze more when alone compared to being in a herd, likely due to fewer distractions and social obligations. However, these findings are based on brief observational periods. A long-term study could reveal different dynamics, as sheep might exhibit varying grazing intensities over extended periods. Our insights are also limited by the dataset scope, which did not capture extended durations or include variables like environmental shifts and detailed social interactions.

Figure 2: Qualitative outputs of our framework depicting resting frames with segmentation masks delineating individual sheep in a state of repose.

Figure 3: UMAP representation of the unique resting pattern of the animals in herd vs single.

In Figure 3, the UMAP scatterplots display the resting postures of sheep from both front and side views. Each data point represents an individual instance of a sheep's resting posture. The clustering of points indicates patterns of similarity: tighter clusters suggest more consistent and homogeneous behavior patterns.


The left plot, showing the front view, and the right plot, showing the side view, reveal that sheep tend to maintain a consistent resting posture when alone, evidenced by the tightly clustered gray points. This homogeneity is likely due to the absence of external stimuli, leading to uniform resting positions.


In contrast, the brown clusters, representing sheep resting in a herd, show a more scattered distribution. This indicates a greater diversity in resting postures, potentially due to social dynamics within the herd or spatial constraints. The scattered clusters suggest that sheep in a herd may change their resting positions more frequently or adopt different postures compared to when they are alone.


These UMAP visualizations provide a compelling visual summary of the differences between solitary and social resting states in sheep. This insight is crucial for understanding sheep behavior in controlled environments and has significant implications for animal welfare and livestock management. The findings align with our observations of grazing behaviors, reinforcing the notion that sheep display more uniform behaviors when solitary. Such insights are pivotal for enhancing livestock management and welfare practices in animal husbandry.

Conclusion

Our innovative multimodal AI-based vision framework, AnimalFormer, integrates cutting-edge models to deliver comprehensive behavioral analytics tailored for precision livestock farming. By analyzing a sample sheep dataset, we demonstrated the framework's capability to provide detailed insights into various activities, including running, grazing, and resting. This framework transcends traditional monitoring methods by offering non-invasive, in-depth behavioral analysis without the need for additional hardware like RFID tags. This not only reduces stress on animals but also ensures more accurate data collection, leading to better health and productivity outcomes.
